## Project Overview

This project examines how labour-oriented social farming can create inclusive work environments for people with severe disabilities (PwDs). Based on qualitative case studies of two Japanese social farms, it analyzes staff attitudes and practices that support PwDs’ meaningful participation in agricultural work. Using participant observation and semi-structured interviews with 14 farm staff members, followed by inductive thematic analysis, the study identifies strategies that foster teamwork, professionalism, a sense of belonging, and long-term vocational engagement. By focusing on labour-oriented social farming, an underexplored area in the literature, this project contributes empirical insights into inclusive, strengths-based work practices for PwDs.

## Data Sources

The data consist of verbatim transcripts from semi-structured interviews and participant observations conducted at two case-study social farms (Farm A and Farm B). Interviewees were direct support staff members who worked closely with people with disabilities (PwDs) in their daily work and held managerial roles within each work unit.

Participant recruitment was coordinated through key managerial staff at each farm, who provided a list of section managers (e.g., vineyard, cellar, winemaking, community garden, rice field). In total, 14 staff members were interviewed: eight from Farm A and six from Farm B. All invited individuals agreed to participate.

Data were collected between November 2020 and November 2021. Interviews lasted approximately 40–90 minutes and were conducted either in person or online, depending on site-specific COVID-19 restrictions. All interviews were audio-recorded and transcribed verbatim. Prior to analysis and sharing, all transcripts were anonymized and reviewed by the interviewees to prevent internal information disclosure. Ethical approval was obtained from the relevant institutional ethics committee, and informed consent was secured from all participants.

## Methodology & Coding

The study adopted an inductive thematic analysis approach (Braun & Clarke, 2006) to explore how staff attitudes and practices contribute to the creation of inclusive work environments for PwDs.

All interview transcripts were manually coded using VERBI MAXQDA qualitative data analysis software. No artificial intelligence tools were used in the coding process. The analytical process involved several stages:
- Repeated reading of all transcripts to achieve data familiarization
- Open coding to segment the data into meaningful units
- Assignment of descriptive codes to each segment
- Iterative grouping of codes into higher-order themes across cases

This process generated 512 initial codes, categorized as follows:
- Staff attitudes: 230 codes
- Staff practices: 148 codes
- Other relevant topics: 134 codes (including Managerial challenges (40 codes), Work environment profiles (58 codes), PwD profiles (25 codes), Community profiles (11 codes))

Subsequently, 378 codes related to staff attitudes and practices were manually grouped into overarching themes that captured shared patterns across the two farms. Theme development was conducted iteratively and in consultation with co-authors to ensure analytical rigor and coherence.

## Step-by-Step Reproduction

This repository enables conceptual and analytical transparency rather than computational replication. To reproduce the analytical process:

- Review the anonymized interview segments provided in the dataset
- Examine the code list (Japanese and English labels) and code descriptions
- Review how codes are grouped into themes and categories
- Compare thematic structures across the two case farms

While exact replication of qualitative interpretation is not assumed, these steps allow researchers to trace how interpretations were developed from raw data to themes.

### Transparency and Data Availability
The manuscript associated with this dataset (Inclusive labour-oriented social farming involving people with disabilities) is currently under peer review. To preserve the integrity of the review process, author contact information and full citation details are not publicly provided at this stage. These details will be added to the repository upon publication of the article.

The dataset shared in this repository consists of anonymized interview segments, coding results, and thematic structures derived from the analysis. Due to ethical considerations and participant confidentiality, the original Japanese-language interview transcripts are not publicly released. However, access to the original Japanese interview texts may be granted upon reasonable request. Requesting researchers will be asked to clearly state the purpose, scope, and intended use of the data, and access will be considered on a case-by-case basis in line with the informed consent provided by participants.

The data are made available for academic and non-commercial research purposes, with the aim of supporting transparency, methodological rigor, and secondary qualitative analysis.
